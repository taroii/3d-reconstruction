\begin{abstract}
Feed-forward pointmap regression has become the dominant route to dense
multi-view reconstruction: a network maps each image pair to per-pixel
3D coordinates in a shared frame, so that depth, relative pose, and dense
correspondence emerge together. Turning many such pairwise predictions
into a single scene still requires a \emph{fusion} step, handled today
either by a per-scene global-alignment optimization or by a learned
recurrent state. Neither makes explicit \emph{where}, and to \emph{what
degree}, the pairwise predictions fail to admit a globally consistent
reconstruction---a question that is a nuisance for rigid scenes but
central for dynamic ones, where object motion makes a single consistent
rigid geometry genuinely obstructed rather than merely hard to find.
We recast multi-view pointmap fusion as the search for a \emph{global
section} of a cellular sheaf on the view graph, whose stalks carry
per-view geometry and whose restriction maps carry the network's
predicted relative transforms. In this language a globally consistent
reconstruction is the kernel of the sheaf Laplacian, recoverable by a
sparse, graph-native diffusion with a spectrally controlled rate, while
the obstruction to consistency is measured by the first cohomology,
whose mass localizes on exactly the moving and occluded regions. The
formulation subsumes the correspondence graph as a strictly less
expressive special case, yields a principled alternative to the
opaque global-alignment optimizer, and produces an unsupervised
dynamic-region detector as a by-product. This document develops the
formulation and the preliminaries it rests on.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ====================================================================

Pointmap regression has reshaped multi-view 3D reconstruction. In place
of the classical pipeline---detect correspondences, estimate poses,
triangulate, fuse---models in the DUSt3R family~\citep{wang2024dust3r,
leroy2024grounding} learn a single feed-forward map from an image pair
$(I^1,I^2)$ to two dense \emph{pointmaps}, per-pixel 3D coordinates
expressed in a common camera frame, so that depth, relative pose, and
dense correspondence are read off a single prediction. For dynamic
scenes, where object motion breaks the rigid-alignment assumption,
MonST3R~\citep{zhang2025monst3r} retrains on moving content and
$\mathrm{D}^2$USt3R~\citep{han2026enhancing} introduces static--dynamic
aligned pointmaps that warp static pixels by camera pose and dynamic
pixels by optical flow, sharpening correspondence on moving objects.

These models are inherently \emph{pairwise}. Recovering a scene from
$N>2$ views requires a separate \emph{fusion} stage that reconciles the
overlapping pairwise predictions into one globally consistent geometry.
Two families address it. The first keeps a per-scene
\emph{global-alignment} optimization: the original DUSt3R formulation
minimizes pairwise pointmap disagreement over per-view depths, poses,
and intrinsics by gradient descent for a few hundred
iterations~\citep{wang2024dust3r}, an objective inherited with minor
variation by its dynamic successors. The second replaces optimization
with a learned state: Spann3R carries an external spatial
memory~\citep{wang20253d}, CUT3R maintains a persistent recurrent
state that emits each new pointmap directly in a shared
frame~\citep{wang2025continuous}, and SLAM-oriented systems fuse submaps with
loop closures~\citep{murai2025mast3r}.

Across both families, global consistency is enforced \emph{implicitly}.
The optimizer drives pairwise residuals toward zero; the learned state
absorbs multi-view agreement into its weights. Neither exposes the
object that actually controls whether fusion can succeed: a
\emph{principled, computable account of the obstruction} to gluing the
local predictions into one scene. For rigid scenes this absence is
tolerable, because a consistent global geometry exists and the only
question is how fast one finds it. For dynamic scenes it is the crux. A
moving object photographed at two times has \emph{no} single rigid
placement consistent with both views; the pairwise predictions are
locally correct yet globally incompatible, and the incompatibility is
not noise to be averaged away but structure to be measured. Current
fusion either fights this obstruction (rigid alignment, which smears the
error into neighboring static geometry) or hides it (a learned state,
which offers no handle on where consistency genuinely failed).

\paragraph{Our viewpoint.}
The pairwise predictions carry exact algebraic structure that names this
obstruction. Index the views by the vertices of a \emph{view graph}
$G=(V,E)$, with an edge for each pair the network has related. Each view
supplies a local description of the scene's geometry; each edge supplies
a predicted transform relating the two descriptions on their overlap. A
globally consistent reconstruction is precisely an assignment of a
geometry to every view that agrees, edge by edge, under these
transforms---in the language of applied topology, a \emph{global
section} of a cellular sheaf~\citep{curry2014sheaves,ghrist2014elementary}
whose stalks are the per-view geometries and whose restriction maps are
the predicted transforms. We make this identification precise and build
the fusion problem on it. The sheaf is the canonical structure for
integrating local observations into a global state~\citep{robinson2017sheaves};
our contribution is to recognize that multi-view pointmap fusion is an
instance, and that its dynamic-scene difficulties are exactly the
sheaf's cohomology.

\paragraph{What the viewpoint buys.}
Three consequences follow, and they organize the paper.
\emph{(i) Consistency is a kernel.} A reconstruction is globally
consistent if and only if it lies in the kernel of the \emph{sheaf
Laplacian} $\Lap_{\sheaf}$, a sparse positive-semidefinite operator
built from the restriction maps~\citep{hansen2019toward}. Fusion
becomes a graph-native diffusion---heat flow under $\Lap_{\sheaf}$, a
sequence of sparse linear solves---whose convergence rate is governed by
the spectral gap of $\Lap_{\sheaf}$, replacing the opaque gradient
descent of global alignment with an operator one can analyze and
precondition. \emph{(ii) Inconsistency is cohomology, and it localizes.}
When no global section exists, the failure is measured by the first
cohomology $\Hcoh^1(G;\sheaf)$, equivalently by the harmonic
$1$-cochains in the cokernel of the coboundary. In a dynamic scene this
obstruction mass concentrates on the edges and regions where motion and
occlusion make local predictions globally irreconcilable, yielding an
\emph{unsupervised} dynamic-region detector---reaching
$\mathrm{D}^2$USt3R's static/dynamic split from topology rather than from
an optical-flow threshold. \emph{(iii) The sheaf strictly refines the
graph.} A bare correspondence graph records \emph{that} two views are
related; the sheaf records \emph{how}, by carrying the transition maps as
data, and its cohomology expresses global obstructions---scale drift,
loop inconsistency, motion---that a flat union of pointmaps cannot
represent. This answers, in the affirmative and precisely, whether a
graph-structured world model is more expressive than a pointmap pile:
not the graph, but the sheaf on it, is.

\paragraph{Relation to prior structure.}
The closest existing machinery is group synchronization: estimating
absolute poses on graph vertices from relative measurements on
edges~\citep{singer2011angular}, with certifiable solvers over
$\SE(3)$~\citep{rosen2019se} and, paired with a learned depth prior,
over the similarity group~\citep{yu2024sim}. Synchronization is the
\emph{linear, single-stalk} shadow of our formulation---the special case
in which every stalk is one copy of the pose group and the only data are
relative transforms---and it targets the same fusion stage. The sheaf
view generalizes it in two ways that matter for dynamic scenes: stalks
may carry dense per-view geometry rather than a single pose, and the
cohomological obstruction is retained and localized rather than
relaxed away. On the topology side, spectral sheaf
theory~\citep{hansen2019toward,hansen2021opinion} and sheaf-based
sensor fusion~\citep{robinson2017sheaves} supply the operators we use;
our contribution is their first instantiation, to our knowledge, on
dense multi-view pointmap reconstruction, together with the
dynamic-scene reading of $\Hcoh^1$.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=0.15em,topsep=0.2em]
  \item We recast multi-view pointmap fusion as recovering a global
        section of a cellular sheaf on the view graph, with per-view
        geometry as stalks and the network's predicted transforms as
        restriction maps (Section~\ref{sec:recon-sheaf}). The
        construction subsumes both DUSt3R-style global alignment and
        $\SE(3)$/$\Sim(3)$ synchronization as special cases.
  \item We characterize globally consistent reconstructions as the
        kernel of the sheaf Laplacian and give a sparse diffusion solver
        with a convergence rate controlled by its spectral gap,
        providing a principled, analyzable replacement for the
        global-alignment optimizer.
  \item We show that the first cohomology localizes the obstruction to
        consistency, and that in dynamic scenes its mass concentrates on
        moving and occluded regions, yielding an unsupervised
        dynamic-region detector that requires no flow estimator.
  \item We analyze the formulation's two honest failure modes---the
        non-abelian curvature of pose-valued stalks, handled by a
        connection (tangent-space) linearization, and the coupling
        between the evolving reconstruction and the sheaf it induces---and
        validate the resulting method empirically.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}
% ====================================================================

We fix notation for pointmaps and the fusion problem
(Section~\ref{sec:prelim-pointmaps}), recall cellular sheaves and their
spectral theory (Section~\ref{sec:prelim-sheaves}), and assemble the two
into the reconstruction sheaf that the method operates on
(Section~\ref{sec:recon-sheaf}).

% --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Pointmaps and the fusion problem}
\label{sec:prelim-pointmaps}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------

Let $I^1,\dots,I^N$ be images of resolution $W\times H$. A
\emph{pointmap} $X^{n,m}\in\R^{W\times H\times 3}$ assigns to each pixel
of image $n$ its 3D location expressed in the camera frame of image $m$.
For a pair $(n,m)$ the network of the DUSt3R
family~\citep{wang2024dust3r,zhang2025monst3r,han2026enhancing} regresses
$X^{n,m}$ and $X^{m,m}$ together with per-pixel confidences $C^{n,m}$,
from which depth, the relative camera transform, and dense
correspondence follow. Writing $\pi_K^{-1}(p,d)=d\,K^{-1}\tilde p$ for
the back-projection of pixel $p$ at depth $d$ through intrinsics $K$
($\tilde p$ homogeneous), each view $n$ carries a depth map $D_n$, a
pose $g_n\in\SE(3)$, and intrinsics $K_n$, and contributes the
world-frame points
\begin{equation}
  \chi_n(p) \;=\; g_n \cdot \pi_{K_n}^{-1}\!\big(p,\,D_n(p)\big).
  \label{eq:chi}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{The view graph.}
Collect the views as the vertices of a \emph{view graph} $G=(V,E)$,
placing an edge $e=(i,j)\in E$ whenever the network has produced a
pairwise prediction relating views $i$ and $j$ (in practice, pairs
selected by a confidence- or overlap-based scene graph). Each edge
carries the predicted relative geometry on the overlap of the two views:
a relative transform $g_e$, a relative scale $\sigma_e$, and the
correspondence field that identifies which pixels of $i$ and $j$ view the
same scene point.

\paragraph{Global alignment.}
The standard fusion objective reconciles the pairwise predictions by
minimizing a confidence-weighted pairwise disagreement,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{align}}
  \;=\;
  \sum_{e=(i,j)\in E}\;\sum_{n\in\{i,j\}}\;\sum_{p}
  \nu_n(p)\,
  \big\lVert
     \chi_n(p) \;-\; g_e\!\left(\sigma_e\, X^{(e)}_n(p)\right)
  \big\rVert ,
  \label{eq:align}
\end{equation}
over the per-view variables $\{D_n,g_n,K_n\}$ and per-edge variables
$\{g_e,\sigma_e\}$, solved by gradient descent~\citep{wang2024dust3r}.
Two features of \eqref{eq:align} motivate what follows. First, it
couples the variables \emph{only} through the shared correspondence
encoded on edges---it is a sum of edge-local agreement terms---so its
structure is that of a function on a graph, not on an unstructured point
cloud. Second, it presumes a consistent rigid global geometry exists and
seeks it by descent; it has no notion of, and returns no certificate
for, the case where consistency is genuinely obstructed, which is the
generic situation under object motion. The reformulation below keeps the
edge-local structure of \eqref{eq:align} but replaces ``descend on the
residual'' with ``identify the residual's consistent part and measure its
obstruction.''

% --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Cellular sheaves and their Laplacian}
\label{sec:prelim-sheaves}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------

We recall the minimal sheaf-theoretic vocabulary; see
\citet{curry2014sheaves} and \citet{hansen2019toward} for full
treatments.

\begin{definition}[Cellular sheaf]
A \emph{cellular sheaf} $\sheaf$ of finite-dimensional real vector spaces
on a graph $G=(V,E)$ assigns
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=0.1em,topsep=0.1em]
  \item to each vertex $v\in V$ a \emph{stalk} $\sheaf(v)$, a vector
        space of data local to $v$;
  \item to each edge $e\in E$ a stalk $\sheaf(e)$, the data shared on $e$;
  \item to each incident pair $v\trianglelefteq e$ a linear
        \emph{restriction map} $\restr{v}{e}:\sheaf(v)\to\sheaf(e)$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

The \emph{$0$-cochains} $\Cochain^0(G;\sheaf)=\bigoplus_{v\in V}\sheaf(v)$
collect a choice of local data at every vertex; the \emph{$1$-cochains}
$\Cochain^1(G;\sheaf)=\bigoplus_{e\in E}\sheaf(e)$ collect data on every
edge. Fixing an orientation on each edge $e=(i,j)$, the
\emph{coboundary} $\coboundary:\Cochain^0\to\Cochain^1$ compares the two
endpoints through their restriction maps,
\begin{equation}
  (\coboundary x)_e \;=\; \restr{j}{e}\,x_j \;-\; \restr{i}{e}\,x_i,
  \qquad e=(i,j).
  \label{eq:coboundary}
\end{equation}

\begin{definition}[Global sections]
A $0$-cochain $x\in\Cochain^0(G;\sheaf)$ is a \emph{global section} if
$\coboundary x=0$, i.e.\ if the local data agree on every edge after
restriction. The space of global sections is
$\Sections(G;\sheaf)=\ker\coboundary=\Hcoh^0(G;\sheaf)$.
\end{definition}

Equipping every stalk with an inner product makes $\coboundary$ a map of
inner-product spaces with adjoint $\coboundary^{\!\top}$. The
\emph{(degree-$0$) sheaf Laplacian} is
\begin{equation}
  \Lap_{\sheaf} \;=\; \coboundary^{\!\top}\coboundary
  \;:\;\Cochain^0(G;\sheaf)\to\Cochain^0(G;\sheaf),
  \label{eq:sheaf-laplacian}
\end{equation}
a sparse, symmetric, positive-semidefinite operator whose
$(i,j)$ block is supported exactly on the edges of $G$, so that
$\Lap_{\sheaf}$ inherits the sparsity of the view graph. When every
stalk is $\R$ and every restriction map is the identity,
$\Lap_{\sheaf}$ is the ordinary graph Laplacian; the sheaf Laplacian is
its data-carrying refinement. We use two standard facts.

\begin{lemma}[Sections are the kernel; obstruction is cohomology
{\citep{hansen2019toward}}]
\label{lem:kernel}
For a cellular sheaf $\sheaf$ on a graph,
\[
  \ker\Lap_{\sheaf} \;=\; \ker\coboundary \;=\; \Sections(G;\sheaf)
  \;=\; \Hcoh^0(G;\sheaf),
\]
and the first cohomology
$\Hcoh^1(G;\sheaf)=\Cochain^1/\img\coboundary\cong\ker\coboundary^{\!\top}$
is isomorphic to the space of \emph{harmonic $1$-cochains}. In
particular $\Hcoh^1=0$ if and only if every edge residual can be
explained by a global section.
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lem:kernel} is the structural backbone of the method:
recovering a consistent reconstruction means projecting onto
$\ker\Lap_{\sheaf}$, which the heat flow
$\dot x=-\Lap_{\sheaf}\,x$ achieves with a rate set by the smallest
nonzero eigenvalue of $\Lap_{\sheaf}$; and the residual that no global
section can absorb is the harmonic representative of
$\Hcoh^1(G;\sheaf)$, a quantity supported on edges and therefore
\emph{localizable}.

% --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The reconstruction sheaf}
\label{sec:recon-sheaf}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------

We now place the fusion problem of \eqref{eq:align} on the sheaf of
Section~\ref{sec:prelim-sheaves}. Fix the view graph $G=(V,E)$ of
Section~\ref{sec:prelim-pointmaps}.

\paragraph{Stalks.}
The stalk $\sheaf(v)$ over a view holds that view's contribution to the
world geometry. In the simplest \emph{coordinate} model, $\sheaf(v)$ is
the space of world-frame point assignments $\chi_v$ of
\eqref{eq:chi} for the pixels of view $v$; in the \emph{pose} model used
for analysis, $\sheaf(v)$ is the tangent space at the current estimate of
view $v$'s frame and scale, i.e.\ a copy of the Lie algebra
$\mathfrak{se}(3)\oplus\R$ (the generator of $\Sim(3)$). The edge stalk
$\sheaf(e)$ over $e=(i,j)$ is the space of geometry on the shared overlap
of views $i$ and $j$, into which both endpoints restrict.

\paragraph{Restriction maps.}
The restriction map $\restr{i}{e}$ takes view $i$'s local geometry to its
prediction on the overlap of $e$; concretely it applies the
back-projection \eqref{eq:chi} and selects the pixels of $i$ that the
edge's correspondence field matches to $j$. The two restriction maps of
an edge are built so that $(\coboundary x)_e=0$ encodes \emph{exactly}
the per-edge agreement term inside \eqref{eq:align}: a global section is a
set of per-view geometries whose overlaps coincide after the predicted
transforms, which is the definition of a globally consistent
reconstruction. Thus $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{align}}$ in \eqref{eq:align}
is, up to the confidence weights $\nu_n(p)$ folded into the stalk inner
products, the Dirichlet energy
$\tfrac12\,\inner{x}{\Lap_{\sheaf}x}=\tfrac12\norm{\coboundary x}^2$ of
the reconstruction sheaf.

\begin{proposition}[Fusion as a kernel problem; informal]
\label{prop:fusion}
With the reconstruction sheaf above, the minimizers of the global
alignment energy \eqref{eq:align} are the $0$-cochains nearest to
$\ker\Lap_{\sheaf}=\Sections(G;\sheaf)$ in the stalk inner product. When
a consistent rigid reconstruction exists it is a global section and the
energy attains zero; when object motion or loop inconsistency obstructs
consistency, the irreducible residual is the harmonic representative of
$\Hcoh^1(G;\sheaf)$.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:fusion} reframes fusion from descent on a residual
to projection onto a kernel, and it identifies the leftover---the part of
the data that \emph{cannot} be made consistent---with a cohomology class
rather than with optimizer noise. The dynamic-scene method developed in
the sequel reads this class off the harmonic $1$-cochains and attributes
its support to moving and occluded regions.

\begin{remark}[Two honest subtleties]
\label{rem:subtleties}
First, poses live in $\Sim(3)$, which is non-abelian, so the linear
coboundary \eqref{eq:coboundary} is exact only on the tangent stalks of
the pose model; on the group itself the correct object is a sheaf with a
\emph{connection}, whose curvature around a cycle is the synchronization
obstruction~\citep{singer2011angular,rosen2019se}. We work in the
tangent linearization and re-linearize as the estimate updates, a
sheaf-valued Gauss--Newton iteration. Second, the restriction maps depend
on the correspondence field, which depends on the very geometry we solve
for, so the sheaf co-evolves with its section; we treat the network's
pairwise predictions as fixed during each solve and refresh them between
outer iterations. Both subtleties are analyzed in Section~\todo{method}.
\end{remark}

\section{A Proof-of-Concept Experiment}
\label{sec:experiments}
% ====================================================================

The viewpoint of Section~\ref{sec:recon-sheaf} rests on one empirical claim
that no prior theory supplies: that the harmonic representative of
$\Hcoh^1(G;\sheaf)$ concentrates on moving regions rather than smearing into the
static surround or being swamped by loop-closure and drift modes
(Contribution~3). Before committing to the non-abelian machinery of
Remark~\ref{rem:subtleties}, we test this claim in the cleanest setting that can
falsify it: a controlled synthetic scene with a single known rigid moving
object, on which we build the linearized reconstruction sheaf and compute the
harmonic $1$-cochain numerically. The experiment uses no learned network and no
training; it isolates \emph{the sheaf machinery} from the quality of any
particular pairwise predictor, and runs in seconds on CPU.

% --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp-setup}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------

We place $N=14$ cameras on a partial orbit viewing a scene of $90$ static
background points and one rigid object of $24$ points that translates and
rotates between frames. The view graph $G$ is the temporal chain augmented with
a few long-range loop-closure edges, so that cycles---and hence a place for
loop-inconsistency modes to live---are present. We instantiate the
reconstruction sheaf in the pose model: each vertex stalk is the $\Sim(3)$
tangent $\mathfrak{se}(3)\oplus\R$, and the edge stalk over $e=(i,j)$ is
decomposed \emph{per co-visible point}, one copy of $\R^3$ per point, so that a
$1$-cochain carries a residual for each scene point on each edge and $\Hcoh^1$
can localize spatially rather than merely per view. For point $k$ on edge
$(i,j)$, each endpoint pushes its observation through the current pose estimate
to a world point $w^i_k$, giving the base residual
$r^0_{ij,k}=w^i_k-w^j_k\in\R^3$; the coboundary acts by the left-perturbation
Jacobian $B_{i,k}=[\,I\;\,{-}[w^i_k]_\times\;\,w^i_k\,]$, and the sheaf Laplacian
is $\Lap_{\sheaf}=\coboundary^{\!\top}\coboundary$, which is precisely the
bundle-adjustment information matrix. The harmonic representative is the part of
the residual that no pose correction can remove,
$h=r^0+\coboundary\xi^\star$ with
$\xi^\star=\arg\min_\xi\norm{r^0+\coboundary\xi}^2$, obtained by a sparse
least-squares solve (projection onto $\ker\coboundary^{\!\top}$, cf.\
Lemma~\ref{lem:kernel}). We compare the per-point harmonic energy
$E_k=\sum_{e\ni k}\norm{h_{e,k}}^2$ against the per-point raw residual energy
$R_k=\sum_{e\ni k}\norm{r^0_{e,k}}^2$---the quantity bundle adjustment drives to
zero---and score how well each separates the (known) moving points from the
static ones by AUROC.

% --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The obstruction localizes, and survives drift}
\label{sec:exp-results}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/story_localization.png}
  \caption{\textbf{The harmonic $\Hcoh^1$ representative localizes on the moving
  object where the raw residual cannot.} Per-point energy on a scene with
  accumulating camera drift ($0.30$ rad/step), one projection; cyan rings mark
  the ground-truth moving object. \emph{Left:} ground truth. \emph{Middle:} the
  raw residual energy $R_k$ that bundle adjustment minimizes---drift inflates
  static points too, so its brightest points are static and the object is not
  separable (AUROC $0.71$). \emph{Right:} the harmonic energy $E_k$, which
  removes everything a pose correction can explain and lights up exactly the
  moving object (AUROC $1.00$). Energies are min--max normalized per panel.}
  \label{fig:localization}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.62\linewidth]{figs/story_drift.png}
  \caption{\textbf{The cohomological signal is invariant to camera drift; the
  raw residual is not.} Localization AUROC (moving vs.\ static) versus
  accumulating per-step camera drift, mean $\pm$ s.d.\ over $6$ seeds. The
  harmonic energy stays at $1.00$ because drift is, by construction, in the
  image of the coboundary and is projected away; the raw residual decays from
  $1.00$ toward chance as drift grows. The two coincide only at zero
  drift---the regime the dynamic-scene problem is not about.}
  \label{fig:drift}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:localization} shows the central result. With ground-truth
poses both $E_k$ and $R_k$ trivially isolate the object; the interesting case is
\emph{accumulating odometry drift}, under which the static background also
disagrees across views. The raw residual then conflates that disagreement with
object motion and fails to localize (AUROC $0.71$, and its brightest points are
static), whereas the harmonic projection first subtracts off every
pose-explainable disagreement and retains only the irreducible part, which sits
squarely on the moving object (AUROC $1.00$). This is exactly the distinction
Proposition~\ref{prop:fusion} draws: bundle adjustment treats the residual as
noise to be minimized and discards the leftover, while the cohomological reading
keeps it as a class and reads it as scene dynamics. Figure~\ref{fig:drift}
quantifies the robustness across a sweep of drift magnitudes: the harmonic AUROC
is pinned at $1.00$ with zero variance while the raw AUROC decays from $1.00$
toward chance, with a variance band that crosses below $0.5$. The harmonic
signal is, empirically, invariant to the camera drift that defeats the raw
residual.

% --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Stress-testing the boundaries}
\label{sec:exp-boundaries}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------

The clean result of Section~\ref{sec:exp-results} also exposes three structural
vulnerabilities of the estimator: (i)~the global scale gauge of the $\Sim(3)$
coordinate model, (ii)~a reliance on static-scene dominance to pin the remaining
gauge, and (iii)~a coboundary that is only first-order in the pose corrections.
Each is a property of the estimator, not of any pairwise predictor, so each can
be isolated and tested where ground truth is known and a solve costs
seconds---before incurring the confounds of real data. We do so, applying a
mitigation wherever one exists, and then validate the combined system on a scene
that stacks all three stressors (Figure~\ref{fig:combined}).

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/combined.png}
  \caption{\textbf{All boundaries stressed at once.} A scene with three moving
  objects, static fraction $0.56$, accumulating camera drift ($0.45$ rad/step),
  and per-view $\Sim(3)$ scale ambiguity. \emph{Left:} harmonic energy from the
  full solver, concentrated on all three objects (colored rings; AUROC $1.00$).
  \emph{Right:} localization AUROC on the same data under three readings (mean

\paragraph{Scale gauge (closed).}
The coordinate residual $w^i_k-w^j_k$ is not scale-invariant, so the uniform
log-scale direction is a coboundary that contracts the reconstruction to a point
and annihilates \emph{every} residual---the obstruction included---driving the
AUROC to zero. A single linear constraint pinning the mean log-scale removes it.
With the gauge anchored, localization is exactly invariant to a random global
similarity (AUROC unchanged to machine precision; harmonic energy rescaling as
$s_g^2$), and per-view scale drift is absorbed as consistent (harmonic AUROC
$1.00$ against a raw residual at chance). This is the metric-scale gauge of
$\Sim(3)$ surfacing in the spectral picture, and belongs beside
Remark~\ref{rem:subtleties}.

\paragraph{Linearization (controlled by iteration).}
Because object motion enters only through the exactly-computed residual $r^0$
while the linearization is in the pose corrections $\xi$, the ``large motion''
concern attaches to pose-error magnitude, not object speed. A single
linearization already localizes up to large drift and fails only at the extreme;
re-linearizing---the sheaf-valued Gauss--Newton of
Remark~\ref{rem:subtleties}---removes even that, holding AUROC $1.00$ to a drift
of $1.0$ rad/step (where the single solve has collapsed to chance) and
converging in two iterations. The projection suppresses only pose-explainable
disagreement, so measurement noise, which is not, sets the ultimate floor.

\paragraph{Static dominance (sharpened, then extended).}
The static background pins the remaining gauge, so the precondition is not a
bound on the total moving fraction but on the largest single coherent group:
several small objects are each recovered even when together they exceed half the
scene, since none rivals the static set. For one large object the least-squares
solve degrades as its share grows, but a robust (IRLS) reweighting lets the
static majority pin the gauge, holding localization down to a static fraction of
$\approx\!0.55$---the symmetry limit at which scene and object become
indistinguishable. The separation bound a localization theorem must supply is
thus governed by the dominance of the static group, not by the raw moving
fraction.

% --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Toward real data: realism stress-tests}
\label{sec:exp-realism}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------

The tests above assume exact correspondences and i.i.d.\ noise; real pointmap
predictions violate both. We probe the three resulting gaps while ground truth
is still available, and report what the real pipeline must carry as a result.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/realism_corr.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Correspondence outliers.} Localization AUROC (left) and
  precision at the number of moving points (right) versus the per-edge mismatch
  rate (mean over $5$ seeds). A summed energy readout collapses, because
  sporadic mismatches accumulate; a \emph{median-over-edges} readout exploits
  that genuine motion is inconsistent on \emph{every} edge while a mismatch is
  not, and with the robust solve localization is preserved to a
  $15$--$20\%$ mismatch rate.}
  \label{fig:realism}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Correspondence outliers.}
A mismatch at a static point yields a large edge residual that is not motion.
Such mismatches are \emph{sporadic}---a point is mis-matched on only a few
edges---whereas genuine motion is \emph{consistent}, inconsistent on every edge
a moving point touches. A per-point energy that \emph{sums} over edges therefore
accumulates the sporadic hits and collapses (AUROC $0.57$ at a $5\%$ outlier
rate), but reading the energy as a \emph{median} over a point's edges, together
with the robust solve, rejects them: AUROC $\approx 1.0$ to a $15\%$ rate and
$0.98$ at $20\%$ (Figure~\ref{fig:realism}). The residual false positives are
spatially scattered rather than coherent, which a within-image smoothness prior
would suppress.

\paragraph{Structured prediction error.}
Network error is spatially correlated and view-dependent, concentrating at depth
and occlusion boundaries. A coherent, view-dependent error on a \emph{static}
region is---unlike i.i.d.\ noise---cohomologically indistinguishable from
motion: it is an irreducible obstruction that neither the harmonic projection
nor the robust solve removes. Only downweighting by predicted
\emph{confidence}---the per-pixel reliability the network already emits, i.e.\
the weights $\nu_n(p)$ of \eqref{eq:align}---suppresses it. Confidence is thus a
required input on real data, not an optional refinement.

\paragraph{Hyperparameters.}
Localization is insensitive to the solver's settings: AUROC stays at $1.00$ as
the Huber constant, the IRLS and Gauss--Newton iteration counts, and the
gauge-anchor weight are each varied over more than a decade.

% --------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Scope}
\label{sec:exp-scope}
% --------------------------------------------------------------------

This is a synthetic, in-the-clean test: the residuals are computed from
ground-truth geometry rather than from a learned predictor, which is what lets
it isolate the sheaf machinery from prediction quality. It establishes that
\emph{if} the pairwise predictions are clean enough, the harmonic
$\Hcoh^1$ provably localizes scene dynamics and does so with an invariance to
pose drift that the raw residual lacks; the realism stress-tests
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-realism}) further identify what a real pipeline must
carry---a robust median-over-edges readout for correspondence outliers and
per-pixel confidence weighting for structured error. It does not yet establish
that a real pointmap network's residuals meet that bar, nor does it treat
occlusion---which is missing data rather than inconsistency and warrants its own
test. A formal
separation bound for the static-dominance precondition, now characterized and
mitigated empirically (Section~\ref{sec:exp-boundaries}), likewise remains open.
Validating the construction on $\mathrm{D}^2$USt3R predictions for a real
dynamic clip is the immediate next step.
